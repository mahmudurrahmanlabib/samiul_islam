    Introduction

    The Media studies and journalism (MSJ) department mainly consist of students who are studying with the sole intention of earning skills that will help them get and excel at jobs in the film industry, in public relations sector or in news outlets. The MSJ department expected to provide undergrad students with practical tools (skill) which they may effectively implement in their professional lives, e.g. learning how to conduct a research well, learning how to be an effective and powerful communicator and also how to be creative (taught in various graphic design courses). But, above all the most crucial lesson given by such a dept. to an aspiring journalist would be to notice details that would be go unnoticed by the untrained eye; this may be achieved by making fieldwork an important part of the curriculum. Graduates from the MSJ department may go on to work in the film industry, as director of mainstream movies or documentary films; while in university, they are often given opportunities to attend workshops conducted by experienced and professional directors; also earning experience through fieldwork gives them the option to pursure a dream of being a television or radio news reporter or a newspaper journalist.  Some graduates, who have taken lessons on mass communication and were perhaps fond of the subject, may prefer to find employment in public relations. Some other, who were perhaps best at conducting surveys and enjoyed doing research, may choose to work for business journals. Thus an MSJ graduate has a wide range of various professions in media.

    While it is true that people who have subjects such as English, Bangla and Political science in university often go to choose journalism as a profession, most MSJ graduates do not choose professions outside the media industry. Still some MSJ graduate beyond the media industry. Also, we will look into if an MSJ degree holder has an advantage or an upper hand when he/she is competing for a job against for example, someone who has graduated from the English department.

    Finally, it is also worthwhile to look into the employment options for MSJ students who have not yet graduated and who still, in some cases, manage to find full-time jobs with good salaries.

    Overall our research is aimed at the capabilities of the employer. Furthermore, we conduct surveys to determine the level of job satisfaction that an MSJ graduate earns for doing the work, both in terms of the salary they are paid meaningful work that helps them make a contribution for the welfare of the society.

    Research Questions

    The objective of this research is to explore whether employers in the media industry assign more importance or whether they prefer students who hold degrees in Media Studies than they did five years ago. As such, the questions we will be exploring are:

    ●    How much value do employers in the media industry give to an MSJ degree holder?

    ●    Do these employers consider which university a prospective employee has graduated from?

    ●    Do MSJ students have more of a chance than students from other departments to get full-time jobs in the media prior to graduation?

    Objectives

    Through the research questions listed above, we may be able to find:

    ●    The employment opportunities for MSJ degree holders inside and outside of the media industry and outside of it

    ●    How much value lies in an MSJ degree, particularly if an individual wishes to make a career in the media sector?

    ●    Provided that there is a scope for employment before graduation, whether that experience will help in getting better opportunities post-graduation

    Literature Review

    The media industry in Bangladesh is one of the fastest growing sectors in the country and that has a lot to do with the people of our country, who, despite their financial backgrounds are keen followers of national politics and public affairs.

    This, coupled perhaps with the somewhat recent emergence of internet facilities across the country, could be a major reason behind the exponential growth in the media industry, which has created numerous jobs. Nearly 42.0 percent of the population has access now compared to 2.67 percent in 2008 ("Bangladesh leads major", 2017).  Prothomalo.com, the website of one of the country's newspapers, is ranked by Alexa -- an American company based in California that provides commercial web traffic data and analytics -- as the fourth most visited website by Bangladeshis behind only Google, Facebook and Youtube (Top Sites in Bangladesh, n.d.). This is especially interesting considering the fact that its 24/7 news portal was only launched in 2013 (Prothom Alo, 2014). Jugantor's online portal is ranked 6th while Kaler Kantho's online portal is ranked 9th.

    While the emergence of these news portals may be considered slow when compared to the western world, it is perfectly in line with the rate of access to internet in Bangladesh.

    We can also observe what has happened to various media industries in the western world and gauge employment opportunities for future graduates. A report reads that in the United States of America, "in recent years, the Internet publishing industry has seen increases in both the number of people employed and the number of establishments (places of employment, such as a stores, offices, or factories.)" (Burea of Statistics [BOS], 2013).

    That trend has also been seen in Bangladesh and, if things keep going that way, then graduates can expect to find employment in online-based media industry.

    However, there are also some disheartening numbers in relation to the fourth estate, particularly in regards to the death of the newspaper industry as more people seek their news online. While there are many arguments against such a notion and the 'death' may be exaggerated, the report adds: "in industries such as radio broadcasting and newspaper publishing both the number of establishments and employment have decreased" (BOS, 2013). That goes to show that there are certainly challenges to the newspaper industry at the very least and that is backed up by figures showing that the number of employees in the newspaper industry has dwindled from almost 4,15,000 in 2001 to 2,25,000 in 2011.

    Moreover, the same report states that in television broadcasting, the number of establishments has increased over the past decade, while employment has declined.

    Research Methodology

    Given the nature of the research, we will be using qualitative methods of research. While there are numbers and figures of how many MSJ graduates there are and how many have found employment, numbers will not be an apt reflect of the perception that the media industry has of these graduates. Therefore, we propose the following methods:

    Firstly, given the number of MSJ graduates in the media industry, we would request different organizations where they are employed for their employee evaluations. This would help us to understand not only their position within those organizations but also the quality of their work, which is likely to be the most important factor in how they are perceived by employers. This would be done through simple random sampling so that only those in better positions, in other words those who are more likely to have better evaluations, are balanced out by those lower down the ladder.

    Secondly, we would request different newspapers who have already published articles about this topic to share any secondary data that may be relevant with us. That would help us get plenty of raw data which can later be incorporated into our research.

    Next, we will interview PR managers, newspaper editors, directors and producers to better grasp their perception of these graduates. For this method, we would need to use stratified random sampling in order to break the respondents into groups corresponding to the area of the media industry to which they belong. For example, directors and producers will be put into one strata while newspaper editors and PR managers will be put into one strata each. There is also a scope to further break up newspaper editors based on which language their newspapers use.

    Research Sampling

    When choosing the newspaper editors whom we shall interview, we will apply simple random sampling, as it is quite impossible to interview all of them. While choosing newspaper articles or reports that will provide us with secondary data, stratified random sampling is most appropriate, as it will allow us to choose articles from both the most and the least well-known newspapers. Stratified random sampling is once again applicable when choosing which directors/producers we want to interview; this will help us gain an idea of how MSJ graduates are viewed and valued by makers of all forms of movies/films, e.g. makers of A-grade films versus makers of C-grade films. Finally, cluster sampling is most convenient while choosing the PR managers whom we shall invite to proactively take part in our focused group discussions. This means we will simply choose a specific bunch of PR managers to see the matter at hand from their perspective and help us gain a better understanding of the worth of an MSJ graduate as a professional inside the media industry. Thus, we will get to look at the present situation of our research topic from various different points-of-view.

    Scope and Limitations

    We are not exploring the employment options for graduates who have completed their higher studies abroad; we are only considering graduates who have earned their degree from

    We are also talking about only those students who have earned their degrees in media related departments like MSJ, Mass communication etc. we are not talking about those who have earned their degrees in other departments like Bangla, English, political science etc.
